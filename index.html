What Is a VIN Number? The Complete Reference

A VIN (Vehicle Identification Number) is a globally unique 17-character code assigned to every motor vehicle — the automotive equivalent of a fingerprint.

The Vehicle Identification Number (VIN), also widely referred to as a chassis number in the UK and Europe, is a standardised alphanumeric identifier assigned to every road vehicle at the point of manufacture. The VIN standard is governed by ISO 3779:2009 and has been mandatory for all vehicles sold in the European Union and United Kingdom since 1981. Prior to 1981, manufacturers used their own non-standardised numbering systems.

The VIN consists of exactly 17 characters, using digits 0–9 and upper-case letters A–Z. The letters I, O and Q are deliberately excluded to prevent confusion with the numbers 1, 0 and 9. The VIN encodes seven distinct categories of information about the vehicle: its region of manufacture, the specific manufacturer, the vehicle type, the model year, the production plant and a unique sequential serial number.

Where to Find the VIN on a Vehicle

🚗

Dashboard Plate

Stamped on a metal plate on the driver's side dashboard, visible through the windscreen from outside the car. Required by law in the UK.

🚪

A barcode sticker on the driver's door jamb or B-pillar, often including the VIN along with tyre pressure and paint codes.

🔧

Physically stamped or engraved directly onto the chassis rail or firewall in the engine bay. Harder to tamper with than stickers.

Recorded in the DVLA V5C registration certificate. The logbook VIN must match the physical VIN stamped on the vehicle.

🛡️

MOT Certificate

Printed on every MOT test certificate issued after April 2018 by DVSA-authorised test stations.

📄

Insurance Documents

Included on most comprehensive car insurance policies and renewal documents as a unique vehicle identifier.

Positions 1–3 — World Manufacturer Identifier (WMI)

The WMI uniquely identifies the vehicle manufacturer and country of origin. Position 1 denotes the world region (1–5 = North America, 6–7 = Oceania, 8–9 = South America, A–H = Africa, J–R = Asia, S–Z = Europe). Position 2 identifies the specific country within that region. Position 3 represents the division, type or manufacturing category within the company.

For example, WBA identifies BMW AG (Germany), SAJ identifies Jaguar Land Rover (UK), and VF1 identifies Renault (France).

Positions 4–8 — Vehicle Descriptor Section (VDS)

These five characters describe the specific vehicle attributes. Their exact meaning is defined by each manufacturer individually, but they typically encode: vehicle line or model, body style (saloon, estate, SUV), engine type and displacement, fuel type (petrol, diesel, hybrid, electric), transmission type and restraint systems (airbag configurations).

Because the VDS is manufacturer-specific, the same characters will mean different things for different makes. A full VIN decode service cross-references manufacturer databases to interpret these accurately.

Position 9 — Check Digit (North America) / Manufacturer use (EU)

In North American vehicles, position 9 is a mathematically derived check digit (0–9 or X) calculated by applying a specific weighting formula to all other VIN characters. Any discrepancy in the check digit is a strong indicator of a forged or tampered VIN. In European vehicles, this position is used by the manufacturer for internal coding and the check digit standard does not apply, though European VINs are still validated via manufacturer databases.

Position 10 — Model Year

A single letter or number encodes the vehicle's model year on a standardised rotating scale defined by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA), adopted globally. The scale runs: A=1980, then B,C,D…Y (skipping I,O,Q,U,Z) up to 2000, then 1=2001, 2,3…9, then the letter cycle restarts at A=2010. The model year is the year the vehicle was designed for, which may differ from the registration year.

Position 11 — Manufacturing Plant

Identifies the specific final assembly plant where the vehicle was built. The coding is manufacturer-defined. For example, BMW uses letters A–Z and numbers 0–9 to designate different plants across Germany, the UK, US, South Africa and China. Knowing the plant can be useful for identifying region-specific variants or confirming whether a vehicle was assembled to UK/EU specification.

Positions 12–17 — Vehicle Identifier Section (VIS)

The final six characters form the sequential production number — a unique serial assigned to each vehicle as it rolls off the production line at a specific plant. Together with the WMI, this creates a globally unique identifier. No two vehicles produced by the same manufacturer in the same model year at the same plant can share an identical VIS. The VIS is purely numeric in many implementations but may include letters at positions 12–14 for additional variant coding.

UK Number Plates — The Complete Guide

Understanding UK number plate formats helps you verify a vehicle's age, detect fraud, and identify import/export vehicles.

The Current UK Format (Post-September 2001)

The current UK registration plate format was introduced on 1 September 2001 and follows a standardised pattern of two letters, two numbers, and three letters (e.g., AB24 CDE).

AB 24 CDE
  • AA (Area Code)
    Two letters identifying the DVLA local office where the vehicle was first registered. For example, LA–LY = London, MA–MY = Manchester, CA–CY = Cardiff/Wales.
  • 99 (Age Identifier)
    Two digits identifying the six-month period when the vehicle was first registered. March plates = the last two digits of the year (24 = 2024). September plates = year + 50 (74 = 2024).
  • AAA (Random Letters)
    Three randomly assigned letters that complete the unique registration mark. These three letters have no specific meaning and are assigned sequentially within a local DVLA office.

🔤

Prefix System (1983–2001)

Letters A–Y (plus R for non-standard use) at the start, e.g., A123 BCD. The letter changed annually in August. A=1983, B=1984 up to Y=2001. This system allowed buyers to identify a car's year from the first letter alone.

🔡

Suffix System (1963–1983)

Letters at the end, e.g., ABC 123A. Changed annually in January, then August from 1967. A=1963, B=1964 and so on up to Y=1982. Vehicles from this era are now considered classics.

⭐

Cherished & Personalised Plates

Private registration marks (cherished plates) can be assigned to any vehicle regardless of its age. When a vehicle displays a private plate, its registration year may not reflect its true age — always verify via a full history check using the VIN or the underlying DVLA record.

🇪🇺

European Import Plates

Vehicles imported from EU countries will carry foreign plates in their history. A VIN check will reveal if a vehicle has been previously registered in another country — vital as imported cars may have different spec, safety standards or outstanding penalties.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿

DVLA Area Codes

The two-letter area code identifies where the car was first registered: L-series = London, M-series = Manchester, N-series = Newcastle, S-series = Scotland, C-series = Wales. Mismatched regional codes can indicate a plate change.

🔢

Q Plates

A Q-plate (e.g., Q123 ABC) is issued when the age or identity of a vehicle cannot be established — common on kit cars, extensively rebuilt vehicles, or vehicles where documentation is insufficient. Q-plated cars always warrant thorough investigation.

Insurance Categories · DVLA · ABI Definitions

UK Insurance Write-Off Categories Explained

When an insurer declares a vehicle a total loss, it is assigned a category. Understanding these is essential before purchasing any used car.

CAT A
Scrap Only
The most severe category. The entire vehicle — including all parts and components — must be crushed and destroyed. A Cat A vehicle can never return to the road and no parts may be sold or reused. Typically involves vehicles that are safety-critical beyond repair or have been involved in major fires.
CAT B
Break for Parts
The body shell must be destroyed, but salvageable mechanical and non-structural parts may be removed and sold. A Cat B vehicle body can never return to the road. However, an engine, gearbox or other components from a Cat B vehicle may legally be used in other vehicles after professional inspection.
CAT S
Structural Damage
The vehicle has suffered structural damage (to the chassis, crumple zones or safety cell) but can be repaired to a roadworthy standard by a qualified professional. After repair, the vehicle must be re-inspected and re-registered with DVLA before it can return to UK roads. Must be disclosed in any future sale — significantly affects value.
CAT N
Non-Structural Damage
No structural damage to the chassis or safety cell. Damage is limited to non-structural components: bodywork, electronics, airbags or glass. Can be repaired and returned to the road without re-registration, though the Cat N marker remains on the vehicle's record permanently. Still depreciates value significantly and must be disclosed in sale.
⚠️
Important note on Cat C and Cat D: These historic categories were replaced by Category S and Category N in October 2017. Vehicles written off before this date may still carry C or D markers. Cat C corresponds broadly to the current Cat S, and Cat D to Cat N. Always check the write-off date when interpreting older records.

Common Used Car Scams — and How to Detect Them

⏱️ Odometer Fraud (Clocking)

The odometer is rolled back to show lower mileage, making the car appear better maintained and more valuable. Signs include: worn steering wheel, pedals or seats inconsistent with stated mileage; MOT mileage readings that decrease or plateau; digital odometers that look recently reset. A Drivium history check plots every recorded mileage reading to reveal discrepancies instantly.

💳 Undisclosed Outstanding Finance

The seller owes money on the car via PCP, HP or a personal loan secured against the vehicle. They sell it to you, disappear, stop paying — and the finance company comes to repossess the car from you. This is one of the most common forms of used car fraud. Always confirm the vehicle is finance-free before any money changes hands.

✂️ Cut and Shut

Two or more written-off vehicles are welded together to create a single car that appears intact. These are extremely dangerous as they crumple unpredictably in a collision. Detection requires a physical inspection of all VIN stamps (dashboard, door jamb, engine bay must all match) and a history check for write-off status on all VINs present.

🚗 Stolen Vehicle Resale

A stolen vehicle is given cloned or forged plates and documentation from a legitimate car of the same make, model and colour. The VIN is a primary defence — even with cloned plates, the physical chassis VIN should match the V5C logbook and the Drivium database cross-reference. Police can seize a stolen vehicle from any owner, regardless of innocence.

📋 Hidden Write-off History

A Cat S or Cat N vehicle is repaired and sold without disclosing the write-off status. While legal to sell if repaired, non-disclosure is fraudulent. Category S vehicles with structural repairs may fail in subsequent collisions due to improper repair quality. Always ask directly and verify independently via a history check.

🌊 Flood Damage Concealment

Water-damaged vehicles are dried out, re-carpeted and polished for resale. Flood damage causes long-term electrical faults, corrosion and mould growth that may not appear for months. Look for high water marks on door cards, condensation inside light clusters, rust on bolts under the bonnet and an unusual smell. MOT advisories for corrosion can also be a historical indicator.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything You Need to Know

Comprehensive answers to the most common questions about VIN numbers, vehicle history checks and the UK used car market.

What is a VIN number and what does it stand for?

VIN stands for Vehicle Identification Number. It is a standardised 17-character alphanumeric code assigned to every motor vehicle at the point of manufacture under ISO standard 3779. In the UK and Europe it is also commonly called a chassis number. The VIN uniquely identifies each vehicle and encodes information about the manufacturing country, manufacturer, vehicle type, model year, production plant and production serial number.

The VIN has been mandatory for all vehicles sold in the UK and EU since 1981. Prior to 1981, manufacturers used their own non-standardised numbering systems, meaning pre-1981 vehicles may have shorter or differently formatted identifiers.

How many characters is a VIN number?

A standard modern VIN has exactly 17 characters. These can be digits (0–9) or upper-case letters (A–Z), but the letters I, O and Q are always excluded to prevent confusion with the numbers 1, 0 and 9. A VIN with any of these letters is invalid. A VIN that is shorter or longer than 17 characters for a post-1981 vehicle is also invalid and should be investigated.

Where can I find the VIN number on my car?

The VIN appears in several locations on every vehicle: (1) Dashboard plate — a small metal plate on the driver's side of the dashboard, visible through the windscreen from outside. (2) Driver's door jamb — a barcode sticker on the door jamb or B-pillar. (3) Engine bay — stamped directly onto the firewall or chassis rail. (4) V5C logbook — printed in the DVLA registration document. (5) MOT certificate — included since 2018. (6) Insurance documents — on most comprehensive policies.

If the VIN in any of these locations doesn't match, this is a serious red flag indicating potential tampering, VIN cloning or a "cut and shut" vehicle.

Is a free vehicle check sufficient, or do I need a paid check?

The DVSA offers a free, publicly accessible MOT history check at check.mot.api.gov.uk. This shows MOT test dates, results, mileage readings and advisory items — which is genuinely useful for detecting clocking. However, it does not cover: outstanding finance, stolen vehicle status, insurance write-off history, number of previous keepers, plate changes, import markers or technical specifications.

For a complete picture that protects you against all major risks, a paid check is essential. At £0.99 on Drivium, the cost is negligible compared to the financial risk of buying a car with undisclosed finance or a stolen vehicle.

What is the difference between a VIN decoder and a vehicle history check?

A VIN decoder interprets the static information encoded in the VIN characters themselves — manufacturer, country of origin, model year, engine type and production serial number. This information is fixed at manufacture and cannot change.

A vehicle history check goes beyond the VIN structure and queries live and historical databases — DVSA MOT records, DVLA registration data, police stolen registers, finance encumbrance databases and insurance industry write-off registers — to reveal what has happened to the vehicle throughout its life. Drivium performs both: it decodes the VIN and runs a full history check simultaneously.

What does 'outstanding finance' mean on a car check?

Outstanding finance means the vehicle was purchased using a hire purchase (HP), personal contract purchase (PCP) or similar finance agreement under which the lender retains legal ownership of the vehicle until the final payment is made. If the seller has not repaid the finance, they do not legally own the car and cannot sell it to you with clean title.

Under the Hire Purchase Act 1964, private buyers who purchase in good faith without knowledge of the finance may have some legal protection. However, trade buyers (dealers) generally do not enjoy this protection and the finance company can repossess the vehicle. The safest approach is always to confirm there is no outstanding finance before purchase using a service like Drivium.

What does it mean if a car has been 'clocked'?

A clocked car has had its odometer (mileage counter) illegally tampered with to show a lower reading than the vehicle has actually travelled. This is done to increase the car's perceived value — lower mileage typically commands a higher price. Clocking is a criminal offence under the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 and can result in prosecution.

Detection: A vehicle history check compares all officially recorded mileage readings from MOT tests, VOSA inspections and service records. Any chronological decrease in mileage, implausibly flat period or statistical anomaly flags a potential clocking event. Physical signs include excessive wear on pedals, gear knob and steering wheel compared to claimed mileage.

How do I know if a car has been in an accident?

Accident history is revealed through several data sources in a comprehensive vehicle check: (1) Insurance write-off markers — if the insurer declared it a total loss, this is recorded on the vehicle's register. (2) MOT advisories — corrosion, structural damage and repairs are often noted by MOT testers. (3) Mileage anomalies — gaps in MOT testing history may indicate a period off-road following an accident. (4) Keeper changes — a sudden keeper change following an accident is common.

Physical checks during a pre-purchase inspection should also look for paint colour inconsistencies, misaligned body panels, overspray and non-matching weld seams — all indicators of bodywork repairs.

What is ULEZ and how do I check if my car is compliant?

ULEZ (Ultra Low Emission Zone) is an emissions-based charging zone in London (expanded in August 2023 to cover all 32 London boroughs) and exists in several other UK cities. Non-compliant vehicles are charged a daily fee. ULEZ compliance is based on the vehicle's Euro emission standard: petrol cars must meet Euro 4 (generally post-2006) and diesel cars must meet Euro 6 (generally post-September 2015).

A Drivium check includes the vehicle's official Euro emission standard and CO₂ data, allowing you to confirm ULEZ compliance. TfL also offers a free ULEZ checker on their website using the registration plate.

What is a manufacturer recall and how do I check if my car has one?

A manufacturer recall is an official notification from a vehicle manufacturer to owners that a specific defect has been identified in their vehicle that poses a safety or emissions risk. Recalls are administered in the UK by the DVSA and are carried out free of charge at authorised dealers.

Outstanding recalls mean the vehicle has a known, unfixed safety issue. Common recall causes include airbag defects (e.g., Takata), fuel system issues, steering failures and software problems. Does a private sale provide the same consumer protections as buying from a dealer?

No. Private sales are governed by the principle of caveat emptor (buyer beware). While the seller cannot make fraudulent misrepresentations (which would be a criminal offence), they are not legally required to disclose all defects, and the Consumer Rights Act 2015 does not apply. You cannot generally return a car bought privately because it later develops a fault.

Dealer sales are covered by the Consumer Rights Act 2015, which requires vehicles to be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose and as described. You have the right to a refund within 30 days for fundamental problems, and the right to one repair attempt by the dealer. This makes a vehicle history check even more important for private sales where you have less legal protection.

How does a VIN check differ from a number plate check?

Both a VIN check and a number plate check query the same vehicle history databases, but they use different identifiers as the lookup key. A number plate check is most common for standard UK-registered vehicles where the plate is known. A VIN check is used when: the plate is unknown or suspected of being changed; the vehicle is a grey import with a foreign plate; the seller's V5C shows a different plate than the vehicle displays; or you want to cross-verify that the plate and VIN match in DVLA records.
